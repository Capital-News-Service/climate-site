As global warming continues to increase the atmosphere’s temperature, cold snaps persist in cities like Baltimore and won’t vanish anytime soon.

There are common misconceptions about the relationship between the warming of the atmosphere and the frequency of cold snaps. A warmer climate doesn’t cause extreme cold weather to become extinct, said Judah Cohen, the Director of Seasonal Forecasting at Atmospheric and Environmental Research.

Days that dipped below 20 degrees and 10 degrees, however, do appear to be occurring at a less frequent rate.

Freezing days haven’t gone away because of the climate change effects on the polar vortex. The polar vortex consists of air masses located above the North and South Pole that contain trapped cold air.

Due to the warming of the stratosphere — the layer of the atmosphere that lies above the one we live in — the polar vortex weakens and can break into multiple sections. This allows the cold air that was once contained in the vortex to escape the poles and move extreme cold temperatures to lower latitudes, such as the continental United States.

What experts do know is that sporadic winter weather will continue, including in the city of Baltimore. Over the past 30 years, there’s been a consistent appearance of cold snaps in the Northeastern U.S. In the years to come, the pattern is not expected to change, according to Cohen.
